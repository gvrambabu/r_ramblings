

Prompt:

I want you to generate a detailed 3-page executive- and architect-ready strategic document describing the modernization of FICC trade lifecycle management for a large broker-dealer. The document should be written from the CIO’s point of view, articulating vision, business rationale, operational need, and future growth, not just technology principles. Do not mention specific technologies or vendors, but focus on architecture, functional layers, and capabilities.

The document should include the following dimensions:

1. Executive Vision and Business Rationale:
	•	Why the current trade lifecycle systems are insufficient for complex derivatives, nested trades, multi-leg swaps, FX options, corporate credit events, month-end treasury rolls, and peak-event bursts.
	•	The commercial, operational, and regulatory imperatives driving modernization.
	•	How the modernization positions the firm for future growth, new products, and regulatory readiness.

2. Trade Data Architecture Concepts:
	•	Trade Event: Granular, immutable events representing all booking, amendments, allocations, barrier triggers, and settlements.
	•	Parametric Trade: Minimal, economic-centric, versioned representation capturing trade economics, supporting deterministic reconstruction, and nested/linked trades.
	•	Fat Trade: Operational snapshot combining Parametric Trade with workflow flags, allocations, and derived cashflows for dashboards, PnL, blotters, and reporting.
	•	Bi-temporality: Transaction Time vs Valid Time and how it ensures historical reconstruction.
	•	Transactional atomicity for economic-impacting events and asynchronous propagation for operational updates.
	•	Examples of complex event sequencing, including: partial allocations, month-end treasury rolls, multi-leg swaps, nested derivative structures, FX options with barrier triggers, and corporate credit events.

3. FICC Desk Constructs and Operational Needs:
	•	Role of Interest Rate, FX, Credit, and Treasury desks in accessing Trade Events, Parametric Trades, and Fat Trades.
	•	How Parametric Trades are versioned when economic parameters change, and when Fat Trades are consumed for operational purposes.
	•	Examples of nested trades and linked derivatives, illustrating multi-leg and FX-IRS hybrid scenarios.
	•	Peak event volume scenarios, burst handling, and deterministic replay requirements.

4. AI-Native and Automation Considerations:
	•	Conceptual description of agentic constructs such as MCP intents and A2A interactions to monitor events, suggest actions, and execute pre-approved operations.
	•	Use of predictive modeling for trade lifecycle events (barrier triggers, FX volatility events, treasury roll exposures).
	•	How AI-native orchestration improves operational efficiency, risk management, and proactive trade monitoring.

5. Legacy System Retirements and Modernization Benefits:
	•	Limitations of current fragmented, monolithic systems.
	•	Benefits of decoupling economic representation from operational reporting.
	•	Scalability, throughput, and peak-event handling advantages.
	•	Regulatory and audit readiness, deterministic replay, and commercial agility.

6. Non-Functional Requirements (NFRs):
	•	High-throughput and low-latency event capture.
	•	Deterministic replay of trade lifecycle for audit and compliance.
	•	Bi-temporal accuracy for historical and business-effective views.
	•	Support for nested and linked derivative structures.
	•	Resilience during peak-event bursts.
	•	Operational layer optimized for query, filtering, reporting, and analytics without impacting core economic consistency.

7. Illustrative Examples and Scenarios:
	•	Walkthrough of a trade lifecycle, showing side-by-side Trade Event, Parametric Trade, and Fat Trade, including when versions are created, and who consumes each object at each stage.
	•	Nested FX Option and IRS swap example showing barrier triggers, partial allocations, and multi-leg dependencies.

Style & Tone:
	•	Written as a strategic 3-page executive document suitable for CIO, board, and senior technology leadership.
	•	Confident, business-driven, and forward-looking; emphasizes why modernization is critical, not just how.
	•	Avoid listing specific vendors or products; instead describe architecture patterns, functional capabilities, and operational benefits.
	•	Include examples, complex scenarios, and NFR expectations to make it credible to detailed-oriented stakeholders.
